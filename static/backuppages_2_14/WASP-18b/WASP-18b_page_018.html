                    This planet completes one year in just 23 hours. Webb telescope has now found water on it

                    During the planet's transit behind its star and subsequent reappearance, the James Webb Space Telescope detected water vapor in its atmosphere.

                    In Short

                    • It was first discovered in 2009 and is an ultra-hot gas giant
                    • It is over 10 times more massive than Jupiter
                    • What sets it apart is its astonishingly fast revolution around its star

                    The James Webb Space Telescope has captured a remarkable sight, a distant planet outside our solar system that has captivated astronomers with the presence of water vapor in its atmosphere.

                    Known as WASP-18 b, this planet is located approximately 400 light-years away from Earth. It was first discovered in 2009 and is an ultra-hot gas giant, over 10 times more massive than Jupiter. What sets it apart is its astonishingly fast revolution around its star, completing one orbit in just 23 hours, in contrast to the 365 days it takes for Earth to circle once around the Sun.

                    advertisement

                    During the planet's transit behind its star and subsequent reappearance, the James Webb Space Telescope detected water vapor in its atmosphere. By creating a temperature map of the planet's movements around the star, scientists were able to analyze the combined light from both the star and the planet. They refined their measurements using only the star's light when the planet passed behind it.

                    With the help of Webb's Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless Spectrograph (NIRISS), astronomers focused their observations on WASP-18 b for approximately six hours. NASA explained that the planet always faces its star, much like how the same side of the Moon always faces Earth. The temperature map reveals a significant temperature shift of up to 1,000 degrees, ranging from the hottest point directly facing the star to the terminator.

                    Megan Mansfield, the lead author of the paper detailing the findings, expressed excitement about the results, saying, "JWST is giving us the sensitivity to make much more detailed maps of hot giant planets like WASP-18 b than ever before. This is the first time a planet has been mapped with JWST, and it’s really exciting to see that some of what our models predicted, such as a sharp drop in temperature away from the point on the planet directly facing the star, is actually seen in the data!"

                    The research team examined the temperature gradients across the day side of the planet and inferred that there might be an impediment preventing efficient heat redistribution to the night side, possibly related to hindered winds.

                    Also Read | When will Gaganyaan Mission launch? Isro chief answers

                    Co-author Ryan Challener speculated on the lack of east-west winds shown in the brightness map of WASP-18 b, suggesting atmospheric drag as a possible explanation. He added, "One possible explanation is that this planet has a strong magnetic field, which would be an exciting discovery!"

                    Astronomers also analyzed temperature changes at different altitudes within the gas giant's atmosphere. They observed temperature increases varying by hundreds of degrees with increasing elevation. Notably, despite the planet's extreme temperatures reaching nearly 2,700 degrees Celsius, the observations confirmed the presence of water features.

                    advertisement

                    Anjali Piette, a postdoctoral fellow at the Carnegie Institution for Science, remarked, "Because the water features in this spectrum are so subtle, they were difficult to identify in previous observations. That made it really exciting to finally see water features with these JWST observations."
